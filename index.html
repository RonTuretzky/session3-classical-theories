Welcome. Tonight is Phase II of the course — theory. The frame for the whole night is simple: every commune in history inherited a toolkit from the ones before it, added its own tools, and handed the kit forward. We're not going to study failures. We're going to study a 500-year apprenticeship.

The frame for tonight

Every later commune inherits
from every earlier commune.

Not "what worked / what failed" —

This is the explicit reframe. The history of communalism is usually taught as a catalogue of disappointments. That's a misreading. Each experiment added durable structural innovations — task rotation, recallable delegates, mutual criticism, common dining, the Minimum, the labor credit, federation — that the next experiment inherited. Tonight is the inheritance, not the body count.

Pre-history

Plato · c. 428–348 BCE

Athens · The Republic, Book V (c. 375 BCE)

"Friends have all things in common."— Plato, Republic IV, 424a

Plato was the first to ask, in writing, the question every commune still answers: who shares what, at what scale, and what binds them?

Bequeathed: common meals as the basic unit of communal life. The thread runs through Spartan syssitia → monastic refectories → Fourier's grand hall → kibbutz dining hall → cohousing common house.

Refs: Wikipedia · Full text (Project Gutenberg) · SEP entry

Plato's communalism was for the guardian class only — not universal. But the thought-experiment matters: he asked the questions. Common dining is the lasting structural innovation that comes through every later tradition.

Pre-history

Thomas More · 1478–1535

London · Utopia (1516)

"Wherever you have private property, and money is the measure of all things, it is hardly ever possible for a commonwealth to be governed justly or happily."— More, Utopia, Book I

Coined the word and the genre. The fictional travelogue as design document — every later utopian writer (Bacon, Cabet, Bellamy, Le Guin) inherits the form.

Bequeathed: rotation between agricultural and craft labor — the direct ancestor of Fourier's "butterfly principle," Marx's hunter/fisherman, kibbutz toranut, and Twin Oaks' labor credits.

Refs: Wikipedia · Full text · SEP

More's structural inheritance: 6-hour workday, 54 nearly-identical cities, housing reassigned every ten years, two-year rotations between city and country. The form is the message — a fully-imagined alternative society, written down so others can argue with it and try to build it.

Pre-history

Winstanley & the Diggers · 1649

St George's Hill, Surrey · April 1, 1649

"The Earth was made by Almighty God to be a Common Treasury of livelihood to whole mankind, in all his branches, without respect of persons."— Gerrard Winstanley, The True Levellers Standard Advanced, April 1649

Ten weeks after Charles I was beheaded, Winstanley led a small band onto common land and started planting parsnips and beans. Within weeks: nine other Digger camps across England.

Bequeathed: the prefigurative occupation — don't wait for the revolution, plant beans on the hill now. Lineage runs through 19th-century homesteading → 1960s land trusts → Greenham Common → ZAD Notre-Dame-des-Landes.

Refs: Wikipedia · Winstanley's Works (OLL) · Marxists.org archive

The Diggers were broken up within a year by local landowners and hired thugs. They are the high-risk pole of the spectrum: no legal title, no shared religious bond strong enough to survive private violence. But the gesture — direct action on common land — is the founding move of every land-occupation tradition since.
Utopian Socialists

The 19th-century designers

Faced with the visible misery of early factory life, they did the most radical thing imaginable: they sat down and drew the alternative in detail.

The "utopians" is a label Marx and Engels stuck on this group to dismiss them. We'll use it without apology — these are the people who first said "we could just live differently" loud enough that other people built things. Their toolkit is enormous.

Utopian Socialist

Robert Owen · 1771–1858

Welsh saddler's son · cotton mill manager at 19 · coined "socialism" in 1827

"Any general character, from the best to the worst, from the most ignorant to the most enlightened, may be given to any community, even to the world at large, by the application of proper means."— Owen, A New View of Society, First Essay (1813)
Owen proved that humane industrial labor is more profitable than brutal industrial labor. New Lanark made money for 25 years and was visited by 20,000 people. This single empirical fact armed every cooperative movement since.

Refs: Wikipedia · A New View of Society (full) · Britannica

Owen ran New Lanark (1800-1825) as Europe's most-visited factory. Tsar Nicholas I, princes of Austria, 20,000 visitors signing the book over a decade. He was a paternalist, but the empirics are real: shorter hours, no child labor under 10, the first infant school in Britain, an Institute for the Formation of Character — and the mills were profitable. This is the foundational proof point.

New Lanark, Scotland · 1800–1825 (Owen) · UNESCO 2001

New Lanark on the River Clyde — UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2001. Photo via Wikimedia Commons · CC BY-SA 3.0

Don't romanticize the paternalism — Owen still owned the mill. But the empirical record is: better conditions plus better education plus continued wages during stoppages produced higher productivity and lower turnover. This is the demonstration that opens the entire cooperative tradition.

Utopian Socialist

Charles Fourier · 1772–1837

French clerk · self-taught · coined the word féminisme (1837)

"The extension of the privileges of women is the fundamental cause of all social progress."— Fourier, Théorie des quatre mouvements (1808), Note H

Lived alone in cheap rented rooms in Lyon and Paris with potted flowers and cats. For twenty years he advertised in newspapers that he'd be home at noon to receive any wealthy patron. No one ever came.

Bequeathed: passional attraction (assign work by what people are drawn to), short rotations (the butterfly principle, max 2h per task), and the Minimum — guaranteed subsistence regardless of contribution. The first systematic UBI, 150 years early.

Refs: Wikipedia · Selected works · SEP

The man himself sounded insane (he believed the seas would turn to lemonade) but the structural ideas are everywhere. Labor rotation is in every commune. The Minimum is essentially UBI. Even his architectural prescriptions — the rue-galerie, the indoor-street — became the shopping arcade and the modern indoor mall.
Diagram

Fourier's phalanstère

A single building for exactly 1,620 souls — 810 personality types × 2 sexes.

Direct architectural ancestor of the kibbutz dining hall layout, Le Corbusier's Unité d'Habitation, and the modern shopping arcade.

Fourier specified the building in obsessive detail. Wings for noisy and quiet activities. The rue-galerie — heated, glass-roofed corridors connecting everything so you never need a coat — is the direct ancestor of the indoor mall and arcade. Le Corbusier explicitly cited Fourier when designing the Unité d'Habitation in 1947.

Utopian Socialist

Henri de Saint-Simon · 1760–1825

Aristocrat → Yorktown veteran → speculator → prophet of industrial society

"The whole of society ought to strive towards the amelioration of the moral and physical existence of the poorest class; society ought to organize itself in the way best adapted for attaining this end."— Saint-Simon, Nouveau Christianisme (1825), Second Dialogue

Hired Auguste Comte (the future founder of sociology) as his secretary. His disciples surveyed the route for the Suez Canal, founded the Crédit Mobilier, and built half the railways of the Second Empire.

Bequeathed: the engineering imagination — that productive infrastructure (electricity, irrigation, transport) is not the enemy of community but its precondition. The agrarian-industrial commune — exactly the form this course is designing — is Saint-Simonian.

Refs: Wikipedia · Selections · SEP

Saint-Simon is the technocratic-industrial current that the other utopians lacked. Where Owen and Fourier built villages, Saint-Simonians built railways and banks. The lasting bequest: a commune needs a productive vocation, not just a residential design. Without industry, communes drift.

Utopian Socialist

Étienne Cabet · 1788–1856

Lawyer · exile · author of Voyage en Icarie (1840)

"Communism is the doctrine of community, of fraternity, of equality, and of liberty. It is the gospel — the good news — presented in conformity with reason, with progress, and with the interests of the people."— Cabet, Voyage en Icarie, expanded preface (1840)

By 1846 there were ~100,000 self-described "Icarians" in French cities, organized in correspondence networks. Arguably the first mass communist movement in history — two years before the Manifesto.

Bequeathed: print as organizational infrastructure. Le Populaire (4,500 subscribers, 1846) is the proto-internet for movement-building. Every later movement — Owenite New Moral World, Fourierist Phalanx, kibbutz federation newsletters, modern community magazines — descends from this.

Refs: Wikipedia · Voyage en Icarie (Internet Archive) · Icarians article

Cabet was authoritarian — he was eventually expelled from his own community in 1856. But the Icarian colonies that *survived* his expulsion lasted 50 years across four states (Iowa, Illinois, Texas, California). The longest-lived secular commune of 19th-century America. The lesson: build founder-removal procedures into the constitution.
Place

Brook Farm · West Roxbury, MA · 1841–1847

Brook Farm print shop, contemporary engraving — Wikimedia Commons · Public domain

Founded by Unitarian minister George Ripley out of the Transcendentalist Club. Members & visitors: Hawthorne, Margaret Fuller, Emerson, Thoreau, Charles Dana, Isaac Hecker.

Equal pay across all jobs (farm = kitchen = teaching). Excellent progressive school. Converted to Fourierism in 1844; the diaspora alumni shaped American letters for the next century.

Bequeathed: self-cultivation as a commune's purpose, not just structural reform. The transcendentalist root passes forward into the back-to-the-land movement, Findhorn, Esalen, and the human-potential commune tradition.
Brook Farm is the genealogical root of "intentional community" with a personal-growth emphasis. When the Phalanstery building burned in March 1846, the colony never financially recovered — but the alumni network became culturally outsized: Hawthorne's novels, Dana's New York Sun, Hecker's Paulist Fathers. Diaspora outlives the commune.
Place

North American Phalanx · Colts Neck, NJ · 1843–1856

The longest-lived Fourierist colony

Founded with editorial support from Horace Greeley, who put Fourierist columns on the front page of the New York Tribune. ~125 members at peak in a 150-foot communal Phalanstery.

Inverse-prestige pay: the unpleasant work paid more, not less. Direct ancestor of modern compensating-differentials and egalitarian wage schemes.

Industrial diversification: grist mill, saw mill, boxmaking shop, the famous "Phalanx Tomato Catsup" sold in NYC.

~30phalanxes in 1840s US
Bequeathed: Fourier's Series and Groups system actually works at scale. Twin Oaks (1967–present) explicitly cites NAP. The kibbutz toranut rotation and Mondragón's compressed wage ratios both descend from the NAP demonstration.

Refs: Wikipedia · The Harbinger (1845–49) · Carl Guarneri, The Utopian Alternative (Cornell, 1991)

NAP is the proof-of-concept for everything that came later in the labor-credit tradition. Task rotation through the day did not produce chaos. Transparent labor accounting did not generate constant resentment. Inverse-prestige pay did not drive away the skilled. These structural innovations passed forward.
Place

The Icarian arc · 1848–1898 · four states · ~50 years

Bequeathed: migration as organizational strategy · schism survival (the 1878 Old/Young split strengthened both successors) · the founding-memoir tradition. Every later movement that relocates, splits, or self-documents follows this template.

Refs: Wikipedia · Icarians · Robert Sutton, Les Icariens (Illinois, 1994)

50 years across 4 states. The Icarians proved a commune is portable — that what makes it a commune is the body of members and the constitution, not the parcel of land. That principle frees every later movement.

Religious Communalists

The lineage that lasted

When secular communes count their lives in years, religious communes count theirs in centuries. The toolkit they handed forward is enormous — and largely unsung.

Religious communalism is the senior tradition. The Hutterites have been at it continuously since 1528. The Shakers since 1747. Modern secular communalism is in many ways an inheritor that has had to reinvent — without theology — the ritual and structural technologies these communities developed first.
"Shakers near Lebanon, NY — their mode of worship," c. 1870 lithograph (Library of Congress).
Wikimedia Commons · Public domain
Religious

The Shakers · founded 1747 · still alive 2026

Mother Ann Lee · Manchester → New Lebanon, NY · peak 6,000 across 19 villages

"Put your hands to work, and your hearts to God."— attrib. Mother Ann Lee, in Testimonies of the Life of Mother Ann Lee (1888 ed.)

Dual-natured Father–Mother God made gender equality a metaphysical necessity. Every village had paired Elder/Eldress, Deacon/Deaconess. The "shaking" was a disciplined collective dance — the worship was the choreography.

Bequeathed: work as practice. Shaker furniture quality came from treating production as formative spiritual work. Every modern maker collective, craft cooperative, or "right livelihood" community translates this into secular form. Plus: gender-paired co-leadership is the structural ancestor of mazkir/mazkira pairs and modern cooperative co-direction.

Refs: Wikipedia · Sabbathday Lake (last living village) · Stephen Stein, The Shaker Experience in America (Yale)

The Shakers are still here. Two members at Sabbathday Lake, Maine, as of 2026, with Sister April Baxter joining in 2025. The society remains formally open to novices. They invented gender-paired leadership, an aesthetic vocabulary the whole world borrowed, and music that's still in the American canon ("Simple Gifts" is theirs).
A Hutterite colony in Martinsdale, Montana. Wikimedia Commons · CC BY-SA
Religious

The Hutterites · founded 1528 · ~58,000 today

Anabaptist · Moravia → Slovakia → Russia → North America (1874) · ~544 colonies in 2026

"Just as there is nothing in our body that our body keeps for itself alone, so there should be no member of the church who keeps anything for himself."— Peter Riedemann, Account of Our Religion, Doctrine and Faith (1545)

Total community of goods, grounded in Acts 2:44–47 — "all things in common" — treated as the permanent shape of Christian discipleship. 500 years of continuous practice.

Bequeathed: the 150-person rule. When a colony grows past ~150 it "daughters out" — half the members move and start a new colony. This is Dunbar's number, arrived at four centuries before the anthropology was written. Used today by the Bruderhof, by federation-design in anarchist communalism, and by every cohousing project that targets 30–80 households.

Refs: Wikipedia · Hutterites.org · John Hostetler, Hutterite Society (JHU Press)

When skeptics say "communism doesn't work at scale or over time," the Hutterites are the standing 500-year counter-example. Combine 16th-century communal economics with industrial-scale modern agriculture. Daughtering-out at 150 is the structural genius — they refuse to grow any colony past the face-to-face commons.

Religious

Oneida · 1848–1881 → silverware brand still operates today

John Humphrey Noyes · Oneida, NY · ~300 members at peak

"Criticism is the great instrument of moral cure. We have found by experience that this is the most effective spiritual machinery for transforming character that has ever been invented."— Noyes, Mutual Criticism (1876), Introduction

Invented mutual criticism — every member periodically subjected to structured group feedback. Industrial diversification (steel animal traps → fruit preserves → silk → silverware). In 1881 they reincorporated as a joint-stock company: the only major American commune that converted rather than collapsed.

Bequeathed: mutual criticism is the direct ancestor of every modern group-feedback ritual — Quaker clearness committees, Kurt Lewin's T-groups (1946), encounter groups, contemporary therapy circles, even Agile retrospectives. Plus: graceful exit via re-incorporation as a model for aging cooperatives.

Refs: Wikipedia · Mutual Criticism (1876, full text) · Mansion House Museum

Oneida is the richest single laboratory in 19th-century communalism. Mutual criticism is the load-bearing inheritance — every modern feedback ritual descends from it. The 1881 conversion to joint-stock with shares proportional to contribution is the cleanest model for graceful exit anywhere in the record. The silverware brand still exists.
Place

Amana Colonies, Iowa · 1855–1932 communal · then joint-stock corporation

An Amana village street in Iowa. Wikimedia Commons

The Inspirationists

German Pietist sect founded 1714 around Werkzeuge — living human "instruments" through whom God still speaks. Migrated to Iowa in 1855 with ~1,200 members on 26,000 acres, founding seven villages.

77 years of full communal property — the longest-running American secular-leaning commune.

The Great Change

In 1932, members voted to separate the religious community (still active today) from the economic community (reorganized as the Amana Society, joint-stock). George Foerstner span out a beverage cooler in 1934 → Amana Refrigeration.

Like Oneida, Amana converted gracefully. The brand carries 19th-century communalist origins into your kitchen today.

Bequeathed: village-scale communal kitchens. Amana's 50+ village kitchens are the structural ancestor of the kibbutz cheder ochel, modern cohousing's "common house meal," and every commune that recognizes shared cooking as the backbone of a commons.

Refs: Wikipedia · Amana Heritage Society

Amana is the cleanest "commune-to-corporation" precedent. The 1932 vote separating church from economy is a textbook governance event that aging cooperatives study today. And the village kitchens — meals served collectively, no household cooking — are the prototype the kibbutz dining hall built on.

Religious

The Bruderhof · founded 1920 · ~3,000 today across 25 communities

Eberhard & Emmy Arnold · Sannerz, Hesse → US, UK, Australia, Paraguay, Germany

"We want to live in such a way that anyone who comes to us would feel: this is the way of the early Christians; this is community of love."— Eberhard Arnold, Why We Live in Community (1925)

Communally-owned firm. Internal: common purse, no salaries. External: a competitive enterprise. Community Playthings (wooden classroom furniture) and Rifton Equipment (adaptive medical gear for kids with disabilities) are the income engines.

Bequeathed: the commune-as-firm. The Bruderhof model — competitive in the market, communal internally — is the prototype every worker-cooperative federation rediscovers: Mondragón, Evergreen, modern platform cooperatives. The answer to "how does a commune fund itself in a market economy?"

Refs: Wikipedia · Bruderhof.com · Plough Quarterly

The Bruderhof translates Hutterite Gütergemeinschaft into 20th-century industrial conditions. Their economic genius: be a firm in the market while remaining a commune internally. Every modern worker cooperative discovers some version of this — the Bruderhof has been doing it since 1920.

What religious communalism teaches the secular tradition

  • Membership filter strength predicts longevity. Centuries of empirical data. The filter doesn't have to be religious — but it has to be something.
  • Daughter-out at ~150. The face-to-face commons has a real upper bound. Don't try to grow past it; multiply instead.
  • Shared ritual outlasts shared rules. Common meals, common worship, common feedback — the rituals carry the community across generations.
  • A productive vocation funds the community. Furniture, silverware, classroom toys, agriculture. The Bruderhof / Oneida / Amana model.
  • Graceful exit is possible. Oneida (1881) and Amana (1932) both converted to joint-stock with shares proportional to contribution. The brand and the network can outlive the commune.
Religion did the binding work. The open question for every secular commune: what plays the role that religion played? Hold the question — we'll come back to it.

Anarchist Communalists

Federation, mutual aid, and the new world in the shell of the old

The tradition that took communalism most seriously as a total social form — neither retreat nor state-capture, but federation from below.

The anarchist tradition is the one that gives the rest of communalism its political backbone. Four theorists, three real revolutions. We'll spend ~50 minutes here. The vocabulary you'll come away with: mutual aid, prefiguration, federation, possession-vs-property, recallable delegates.

What "anarchism" means here

Not chaos. Not "no rules." Not bomb-throwing.

An-archē — without a ruler.
Order without domination.

  • Voluntary association as the unit of social life
  • Federation, not centralization, when scaling up
  • The means must prefigure the ends
"Anarchy is order without power."— Pierre-Joseph Proudhon
Reset the term. Most people walked in tonight with a cartoon picture. The tradition is in fact obsessed with order — just order produced by association from below rather than imposed from above. The "prefigurative" commitment matters: the means must look like the ends.

Anarchist

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon · 1809–1865

Cooper's son · self-taught printer · first to call himself "anarchist" (1840)

"What is the principle of mutuality? Service for service, product for product, loan for loan, insurance for insurance, credit for credit, security for security, guarantee for guarantee."— Proudhon, General Idea of the Revolution, Fifth Study (1851)

Set type for theology and philosophy as a printer's apprentice — taught himself Hebrew, Greek, and the entire reading list of European thought as it passed through his press.

Bequeathed: property vs. possession. Property = the right to extract rent without working. Possession = the right of the user-occupier (the farmer who tills, the family in the house). Every community land trust implements this distinction. Every credit union descends from his People's Bank.

Refs: Wikipedia · What is Property? · SEP: Anarchism

Proudhon is the spine of European anarchism. The property/possession distinction is essential and is exactly what land trusts and most modern cooperatives operationalize. "Property is theft" attacks rentier extraction; the toothbrush isn't theft. Hold both.

Anarchist

Mikhail Bakunin · 1814–1876

Russian aristocrat → Dresden barricade → Peter & Paul Fortress → escape east through Japan

"Liberty without socialism is privilege and injustice; socialism without liberty is slavery and brutality."— Bakunin, Federalism, Socialism, Anti-Theologism (1867)

Fought on the Dresden barricades in 1849 alongside Richard Wagner. Survived two death sentences and six years of solitary in the Tsar's fortresses. In 1861 escaped Siberia eastward through Hakodate, San Francisco, Panama, New York, London.

Bequeathed: the prophetic critique of the vanguard. In 1873 (44 years before October 1917) Bakunin wrote that a "people's state" run by ex-workers and intellectuals will become a new aristocracy of administrators. Every later libertarian-left's immune system against authoritarian "transitions" runs on this insight.

Refs: Wikipedia · God and the State · Statism and Anarchy

Bakunin's escape from Siberia eastward through Japan and the US is one of the great revolutionary biographies. His 1873 forecast of how Marxism would degenerate reads as direct testimony from October 1917 — written 44 years before. The split with Marx in 1872 at the Hague Congress is the original schism in modern socialism.

Anarchist · the centerpiece

Peter Kropotkin · 1842–1921

Prince → Tsar's page at 15 → Siberian field biologist → revolutionary → returned to Russia 1917 → buried Bolshevism in his diary

"The mutual-aid tendency in man has so remote an origin, and is so deeply interwoven with all the past evolution of the human race, that it has been maintained by mankind up to the present time, notwithstanding all vicissitudes of history."— Kropotkin, Mutual Aid (1902), Chapter VIII

Older blood than the Romanovs. Renounced his title at 12. Did fieldwork in Siberia with Darwin in his saddlebag — and saw the opposite of "struggle for existence." Came home with a counter-thesis that took him 40 years to publish.

Refs: Wikipedia · Mutual Aid · The Conquest of Bread · Fields, Factories and Workshops

Kropotkin gets two slides because he's the centerpiece. His funeral procession through Moscow in 1921 was the LAST legal anarchist demonstration in Soviet Russia — banners read "Where there is authority, there is no freedom." Kronstadt erupted three weeks later. He's the rare radical who comes off as kind in his writing — people who met him used the word "saint."
Diagram · Mutual Aid

Kropotkin's argument, visualized

Cooperation is not utopian — it's how life on Earth actually works.

Modern evolutionary biology vindicates the core: kin selection, reciprocal altruism, eusociality — all forms of the cooperation Kropotkin documented in 1902.

The teaching move: this network IS the argument. Every dot is an organism, every line is a cooperative interaction. Kropotkin's claim is that this density of cooperation is the dominant survival strategy within species. Modern biology supports a chunk of this — not all of it, but enough that the framing has vindicated rather than failed.

Anarcho-communism — the positive program

  • Property: abolished as private. Means of production and consumption goods held in common.
  • Distribution: "from each according to ability, to each according to need" (Kropotkin pushes past Bakunin's labor-token version).
  • Governance: direct democracy in the commune; federation across communes by recallable delegates.
  • Scale: communes the size of a quarter or village, federated regionally and globally.
  • Labor: 4–5 hours a day suffices once parasitism is abolished; rotation between manual and intellectual work.

In The Conquest of Bread, Kropotkin literally calculates how many work-hours Paris would need to feed and house itself.
It is a startlingly concrete book.

Read The Conquest of Bread

The Conquest of Bread is the answer to "but what would your society actually look like?" Kropotkin walks through agriculture, industry, housing, clothing, and time-use. He's the rare anarchist with a spreadsheet. This is the book that influenced the Spanish anarchists when they actually had to run things.

Anarchist

Gustav Landauer · 1870–1919

German-Jewish anarchist · translator of Whitman & Kropotkin · Bavarian Soviet culture minister · killed by Freikorps

"The state is a condition, a certain relationship between human beings, a mode of behavior; we destroy it by contracting other relationships, by behaving differently toward one another."— Landauer, Schwache Staatsmänner, schwacheres Volk! (1910)

Best friend of Martin Buber. Through Buber, Landauer's ideas seeded the early kibbutz movement.

Bequeathed: prefigurative politics. Don't wait for the barricade — already be the new society in embryo. Every intentional community, ecovillage, Catholic Worker farm, mutual-aid network — and the COVID-era shift from protest to building — is operating in Landauer's frame: behave differently toward one another, and the state recedes.

Refs: Wikipedia · For Socialism (1911) · Revolution and Other Writings (PM Press)

Landauer is the bridge between European anarchism and the kibbutz movement. His pivot — from "smash the state" to "withdraw from the state by living differently" — is the move that makes communalism possible as a strategy distinct from revolution. We're not planning to seize state power. We're planning to live differently. That's pure Landauer.
Diagram · Federation

Federation: the alternative to the state

Federation is the critical structural concept. Recallable delegates with bound mandates — they cannot vote their conscience, they vote their commune's instructions. The Zapatistas call this "leading by obeying." Rojava implements it today. The Spanish CNT ran it for three years. Different from "representation" — fundamentally different.
Real revolutions

Three proofs at three scales

Anarchist communalism wasn't only theory. It administered millions of people, in three places, in three centuries.

2M / 72dParis Commune (1871) — under siege
7M / 3yFree Territory · Ukraine (1918–21)
7M / 3ySpanish Revolution (1936–39)

All three ended by external military force. None failed internally first.

These are the three proof points. Not 50-person colonies — millions of people running large industrial and agricultural economies. Each was destroyed by armies, not by contradiction. That pattern is meaningful.
Place

The Paris Commune · March 18 – May 28, 1871 · 72 days

The Vendôme Column toppled during the Paris Commune, May 16, 1871. Wikimedia Commons · Public domain

What they built in 72 days

  • Recallable delegates, capped at workers' wages
  • Separation of church and state
  • Workers' management of abandoned workshops (Apr 16)
  • Abolition of night work in bakeries (Apr 20)
  • Free, secular, compulsory education for both sexes
  • Women's Union — first mass women's organization in modern history

Daily life under the red flag

Cafés stayed open. Theatres ran under workers' management. Pawned items under 20 francs returned free. Crucifixes removed from courtrooms. Tipping forbidden. Eugène Pottier, hiding in a Paris attic in June 1871, wrote the lyrics of L'Internationale.

Bequeathed: two million people, under siege, with no preparation, governed themselves for 72 days through directly elected, recallable delegates without descending into chaos. Bread baked. Schools opened to girls for the first time in French history. The Commune is the first proof in industrial modernity that the city is a possible unit of self-government.

Refs: Wikipedia · Marx, Civil War in France · Kristin Ross, Communal Luxury (Verso)

Read aloud: every social innovation the Commune announced — eight-hour day, women's suffrage, secular education, workers' management — has since become normal, often centuries later. They weren't ahead of their time. They were the time.
Louise Michel, photographic portrait. Wikimedia Commons · Public domain
Communard

Louise Michel · 1830–1905

Schoolteacher · "the Red Virgin" · fought with the 61st battalion in men's uniform

"Si je ne suis pas coupable, faites de moi ce que vous voudrez. Si je suis coupable, frappez-moi de la balle."
("If I am not guilty, do with me as you will. If I am guilty, kill me with a bullet.")— Louise Michel, before the 6th Council of War, December 16, 1871

Surrendered to save her mother. Deported to New Caledonia, where she sided with the Kanak revolt of 1878 — extending revolutionary solidarity across colonial lines, decades before this was politically articulated.

Refs: Wikipedia · The Red Virgin: Memoirs

Louise Michel runs an ambulance, fights at the barricades, runs a school, and after her deportation joins the Kanak indigenous revolt against French colonization in New Caledonia. The internationalist lineage of solidarity across colonized peoples starts here, in practice, before it's a theory.

Free Territory

The Makhnovshchina · 1918–1921 · ~7M people · 75,000 km²

Nestor Makhno · Huliaipole, Ukraine · spent 9 years in Butyrka prison reading Kropotkin in irons

"Land to the peasants, factories to the workers, all power to the Soviets of workers, peasants and insurgents — but not to political parties."— Declaration of the Revolutionary Insurrectionary Army of Ukraine, 1919

Three Regional Congresses of Peasants, Workers and Insurgents elected from village and factory assemblies. Voluntary army of ~40,000 with elected officers. Land redistributed on use-rights. Free communes coexisted with individual smallholdings — pluralism within the Territory.

Bequeathed: proof that anarchist administration scales to seven million people across a region larger than Belgium — under continuous war on multiple fronts. The largest pure-anarchist administered territory in modern history.

Refs: Wikipedia · Free Territory · Arshinov, History of the Makhnovist Movement · Voline, The Unknown Revolution

The Makhnovshchina is omitted from most Western histories of the Russian Revolution because it doesn't fit either Cold War narrative. It's neither Bolshevik nor White. It's the largest pure-anarchist administered territory in history and it ran for three years under sustained military assault from multiple directions. Crushed by the Red Army in November 1920 — the moment Bolshevism reveals its character to the libertarian left.
Buenaventura Durruti, c. 1936. Wikimedia Commons · Public domain
Spanish Revolution

Spain 1936–1939 · CNT-FAI · ~7M people in collectivized regions

Catalonia 60% of industry · ~450 collectivized villages in Aragón · 1.5M CNT members at peak

"We carry a new world here, in our hearts. That world is growing this minute."— Buenaventura Durruti, Toronto Daily Star, July 1936

Workers seized factories, trams, telephones, cinemas. Barcelona's tram system — run by 7,000 workers — increased cars in service from 600 to 700 in weeks and turned a profit. Catalonia produced tanks and aircraft from scratch within months.

Bequeathed: proof that anarcho-syndicalist coordination can run a wartime industrial economy. Mondragón Cooperative Corporation (founded 1956, 70,000+ employees today) draws directly on the suppressed CNT industrial-cooperative tradition.

Refs: Wikipedia · Dolgoff, The Anarchist Collectives · Orwell, Homage to Catalonia

Spain is the most studied because anarchists wrote it down and Orwell witnessed it. The Aragón collectives are the closest historical analog to what people in this room are designing — small-scale agrarian collectives federated across a region, in a participating but uncoercive relationship to a wider economy. Read Dolgoff. The economic detail is remarkable.
Witness

Orwell in Barcelona, December 1936

"I had dropped more or less by chance into the only community of any size in Western Europe where political consciousness and disbelief in capitalism were more normal than their opposites. Up here in Aragón one was among tens of thousands of people, mainly though not entirely of working-class origin, all living at the same level and mingling on terms of equality. In theory it was perfect equality, and even in practice it was not far from it... There was much in it that I did not understand, in some ways I did not even like it, but I recognized it immediately as a state of affairs worth fighting for." — George Orwell, Homage to Catalonia (1938), Chapter 1

A first-hand account from a witness who arrived skeptical and left transformed.

Read Homage to Catalonia (full text, free)

Read this aloud. Slowly. It lands. Orwell came to fight fascism and didn't expect to find a society. The phrase "a state of affairs worth fighting for" is the one that should stay in the room.

One example among many

The kibbutz: a 20th-century synthesis

Not the centerpiece — one example of how Tolstoyan anarchism, Kropotkin's mutual aid, and Landauer's communalism converged into a 20th-century communal form that ran at scale.

Brief block. The kibbutz is interesting because it shows what happens when the previous traditions fuse. Tolstoy + Kropotkin + Landauer + labor Zionism = Degania 1910. We'll cover roots, the founding, the structural innovations that traveled, and one honest reckoning slide.

Where it came from — three threads converging

A.D. Gordon

Self-taught Russian estate manager who arrived in Palestine at age 48 and joined the orange-grove laborers. Wrote his "religion of labor" in the evenings by oil lamp.

Ber Borochov

Marxist labor Zionist, 36 when he died. Theorized the inverted pyramid: a community severed from its productive base must rebuild it materially.

Martin Buber

Best friend of Landauer. Through Buber, European anarchism reached Palestine. Called the kibbutz "the experiment that did not fail."

Plus: Tolstoy and Kropotkin were on every founder's reading list.

Refs: Gordon · Borochov · Buber, Paths in Utopia (full text)

Three roots in 30 seconds: Gordon = the spiritual ground (labor as sacrament), Borochov = the political-economic ground (rebuilding the productive base), Buber = the philosophical ground (community as ontological category, federation as form). Plus the heavy reading of Tolstoy and Kropotkin among the founders.
Place

Degania Aleph · founded 1910 · "Mother of the Kibbutzim"

The first house at Degania Alef, on the south shore of the Sea of Galilee. PikiWiki Israel / Wikimedia Commons · CC BY 2.5

Twelve people on a malarial riverbank. Founded as a kvutza — small group, deliberately intimate, no hierarchy. Common purse, common labor, common dining.

Yosef Baratz, twenty years old at the founding, later wrote one of the most readable communal-founding memoirs in print: A Village by the Jordan (1954).

"We were happy enough working on the land, but we knew more and more certainly that the ways of the old settlements were not for us. We thought there shouldn't be employers and employed at all. There must be a better way."— Joseph Baratz, A Village by the Jordan, opening pages
Twelve people. Dunbar-style small group, where consensus is achievable face-to-face. The kvutza model is the original kibbutz. Baratz's memoir is the most accessible primary document of any 20th-century commune founding — reads like a New England farmer's diary.

Structural innovations that traveled

  • Common dining hall (cheder ochel) — descendant of Plato's syssitia, Fourier's grand hall, Amana village kitchens. Proven civic architecture.
  • Labor rotation (toranut) — direct lineage from Fourier and More.
  • General assembly — weekly all-member governance.
  • Federation across kibbutzim — Kibbutz Artzi, Kibbutz Meuhad, regional movements.
  • Industrial vocation — Kibbutz Hatzerim's Netafim (drip irrigation) became a global firm. The Saint-Simonian inheritance.

Each of these features is an inheritance from earlier tradition, integrated into a single working form.

Notice how many of these features are direct implementations of ideas from earlier blocks. The kibbutz is not its own thing — it's a confluence. That's why it's useful to study: it shows the toolkit being applied at scale.

The arc, in one curve

~75 years of fully-collective form, then a long gradient toward differential salaries and renewed-kibbutz models. Both forms continue today.

The kibbutz teaches: a communal form can run at scale for ~75 years and still converge with the surrounding economy when the surrounding economy moves. State support cuts both ways. The form that emerged on the other side ("renewed" / privatized) still preserves a lot — common land trusts, mutual aid funds, collective decision-making — but redistributes individual income.

An honest acknowledgment

The kibbutz movement was integral to the Zionist settlement project. Many kibbutzim were established on or near Palestinian villages whose inhabitants were displaced in 1948. This is part of the historical record. A serious teaching has to hold both truths at once:

The communal form works. Generations of children raised collectively. A productive agrarian-industrial economy run democratically for decades. The dining hall as civic architecture. Federation as economic resilience.

And. The form was deployed inside a settler-colonial project, on land from which others were displaced. The fact of structural achievement does not erase or excuse the colonial context.

Refs: Gershon Shafir, Land, Labor and the Origins of the Israeli-Palestinian Conflict (Cambridge, 1989) · Zeev Sternhell, The Founding Myths of Israel (Princeton, 1998)

Brief, honest, not minimizing, not consuming the block. We hold both truths and move on. For students who want to dig deeper, Shafir is the indispensable text.

The property spectrum

Knowing your position on this axis is one of the first hard decisions any commune has to make.

The spectrum is empirical. Religious communities go further on common ownership than secular ones do. That doesn't mean a secular community can't go all the way, just that historically the religious ones have. The spectrum also clarifies that "commune" is not one thing — it's a position on this axis.

What travels — what every modern commune borrows

Universally inherited tools

  • Common dining — Plato → Fourier → Amana → kibbutz → cohousing
  • Labor rotation — More → Fourier → NAP → kibbutz → Twin Oaks
  • Recallable delegates — Proudhon → Paris Commune → CNT → Zapatistas → Rojava
  • Mutual criticism — Oneida → T-groups → encounter groups → Agile retros
  • Property vs. possession — Proudhon → land trusts
  • The Minimum — Fourier → modern UBI proposals
  • Print infrastructure — Cabet → kibbutz federation → modern community magazines
  • Daughter-out at ~150 — Hutterites → Bruderhof → cohousing scale targets
  • Mutual aid networks — Kropotkin → Spanish 1936 → COVID-19 networks
  • Graceful exit via re-incorporation — Oneida 1881 → Amana 1932 → modern aging cooperatives

Honest constraints to design around

  • Open membership without shared values converges fast
  • Founder dependency creates a succession bottleneck
  • Total childcare collectivization is a hard sell across most cultures
  • Religious bonding is hard to replicate without religion (the open question)
  • Total dependence on a host state moves with the state
  • Single points of failure (one building, one industry) are fragile

These aren't reasons not to try — they're the design constraints reality has already given us.

Left column is the gift. Right column is the constraint. Together: the licensed starting position. Every modern commune is the inheritor of these tools and lives with these constraints.

The deeper question

If religious communities outlast secular ones, and you're building a secular community —
what plays the role that religion played?

Hold this question for the workshop. It's the single hardest design question you'll face.

Some traditions have offered partial answers: explicit ideology, shared aesthetic, shared work, shared practice, shared place. None replaces religion fully. All do some of the work religion did.

Leave the room with this question. It's not rhetorical and there's no clean answer. Some options people have tried: explicit ideology (kibbutz), shared aesthetic (intentional community / Burning Man), shared work (Mondragón — next week), shared practice (zen sangha, permaculture). Your values statement tonight is partly an answer to this.
Workshop

Your values statement

Identify the traditions you align with. Draft the document that will guide every later decision.

We have ~15 minutes. The full draft is homework continuation. Tonight just get something on paper. Pen and paper, please. No laptops.

What a values statement does

  • It's the load-bearing document when you have to make a hard call later
  • It tells a prospective member what they're joining (and not joining)
  • It makes the implicit explicit so disagreement is about substance, not vibes
  • It's shorter than you think — one page, ideally

References to read short, working values statements: Twin Oaks Bylaws · Bruderhof "First Law" · Camphill Pledge

Stress: short. The temptation is to write a manifesto. Don't. The values statement should be readable in 90 seconds and useful in a fight.

Seven prompts

  1. Property: What's held in common? What stays personal?
  2. Decision-making: Consensus, majority, council, sortition? When can someone decide alone?
  3. Membership: How does someone become a full member? How do they stop being one?
  4. Labor: Is work shared, rotated, specialized, paid, unpaid?
  5. Outside world: Are you a demonstration, a withdrawal, a federation node, a state-partner?
  6. Conflict: What's the process when members fundamentally disagree?
  7. Succession: What persists if the founders leave or die?

For each prompt: one or two sentences. Don't aim for completeness; aim for a position.

Seven prompts — the five lenses plus conflict and succession (added because they're where actual communes break). Tell people: pick the one that scares you most and start there.

12 minutes of writing

  • Choose three of the seven prompts — the ones you have the strongest take on right now
  • Write two sentences for each. Position, not justification.
  • Then circle one tradition from tonight that your answers most resemble
  • Then write one sentence on where you differ from that tradition

Pen and paper. Phones off.
12 minutes. Go.

Set a timer. Walk the room while they write. After 12 minutes, ask for two volunteers to read what they wrote. Don't workshop tonight — first articulation, not polished document.
