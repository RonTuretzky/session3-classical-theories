Block 1

The Utopian Socialists

Imagine a better society in enough detail that someone might try to build it.

The "utopian socialists" is a label Marx and Engels stuck on a cluster of thinkers to dismiss them. We'll use it but with respect — these are the people who first said "we could just live differently" loud enough that other people built things. Their tradition has two layers: the imaginative literature (More, Fourier) and the experimental colonies (Owen, Cabet).

Pre-history: three sparks

Plato, ~375 BCE

Republic, Book V.
Guardians hold property and family in common. Communism for the elite, not the masses.

More, 1516

Utopia. Common storehouses, six-hour workday, religious tolerance, slavery for criminals. Coined the genre — and the word.

Diggers, 1649

Winstanley occupies St George's Hill: "the earth was made to be a common treasury for all." Crushed in a year.

Three modes: philosophical, literary, insurrectionary. All three return.

I'm compressing 2000 years into a slide. The point is: communalist imagination predates industrial capitalism by a long way. Plato is communism-for-the-rulers (think: Bezos's bunker). More invents the modern utopia genre and is ambivalent about his own picture. The Diggers are the first European group to *try* it as direct action. Each of these prefigures something we'll see tonight.

Why the 19th century?

  • Industrial revolution → first mass urban poverty in modern form
  • French Revolution had already broken the legitimacy of the old order
  • Printing + literacy made manifestos a viable medium
  • And: cheap land in the Americas to actually try it

The utopians are responding to a specific shock — the visible misery of early factory life — with the conviction that another economy is designable.

Frame this as a moment of crisis-and-possibility, like ours but more so. Visible child labor, 14-hour days, tenement disease. And the political legitimacy of hierarchy had cracked. So: write a plan.

Robert Owen (1771–1858)

Welsh textile manufacturer turned reformer.

Ran New Lanark (Scotland) as a model factory: shorter hours, education, no child labor under 10. Profitable.

Pivoted to "villages of cooperation" — planned communities of 500–1500 people, common property, productive agriculture + light industry.

"Any general character, from the best to the worst, from the most ignorant to the most enlightened, may be given to any community… by the application of the proper means." — Owen, A New View of Society, 1813
Owen is a rationalist — he believes character is environmentally formed, so design the environment and you design the person. This is the seed of nearly every modern "intentional community" pitch. He was also a paternalist factory owner who genuinely believed his workers were better off — which they probably were, by the standards of 1810. Hold both.

New Harmony, Indiana, 1825

  • Owen buys a town from the Rappites for $135,000
  • Recruits 800 settlers via newspaper ads — "all classes of society"
  • No vetting. No shared religion. Constitution rewritten seven times in two years.
  • Collapses by 1827. Owen loses 80% of his fortune.

Lesson logged for every commune since: open membership without shared values is fast collapse.

This is one of the most instructive failures in commune history. Owen's mistake is legible: he believed environment shaped character so completely that he didn't need to filter who came in. He thought the form would do the work. It did not. Pin this to your wall: who you let in is who you become.

Charles Fourier (1772–1837)

French clerk. Self-taught. Strange.

Designed the phalanstère: a single building housing exactly 1,620 people, the optimal number to express all human "passional types."

Labor organized into "Series" — short rotating tasks, chosen by passion, never more than two hours at one job.

Predicted the seas would turn to lemonade.

"The passions, thwarted in their natural direction, like waters dammed up, recoil and produce ravages." — Fourier, Theory of the Four Movements, 1808
Fourier is the patron saint of every weird commune since. His core move is radical: he says human passions aren't sinful or to be suppressed — they're the engine. Design society so that doing what you want at any moment also does the work that needs doing. Match labor to attraction. The lemonade-seas thing is real and is cited every time someone wants to dismiss him. Don't dismiss him. The labor-rotation idea ends up everywhere — including kibbutz.

Fourier's structural ideas that traveled

  • Passional attraction — assign work by what people are drawn to, not by hierarchy
  • Short rotations — no one stays at any task long enough to be ground down
  • The "Minimum" — every member guaranteed food, clothing, shelter regardless of contribution
  • Architecture as politics — a single building shapes a single society

Echoes: Israeli kibbutz dining halls, modern co-housing common houses, UBI, gig-style task variety.

I always teach Fourier with this slide because the man himself sounds insane but the structural ideas are everywhere. Labor rotation is a kibbutz feature. The Minimum is essentially UBI. Architectural determinism is in every co-housing design. He was wrong about a lot and right about more than people credit.

Real Fourierist colonies (1840s US)

  • Brook Farm (Massachusetts, 1841–1847) — Transcendentalist; Hawthorne lived there and hated it; converted to Fourierism in 1844; burned down
  • North American Phalanx (New Jersey, 1843–1856) — the longest-lasting; ~110 members at peak; ended in financial collapse + religious schism
  • ~30 phalanxes total in the US in the 1840s. Most died within 3 years.

The pattern: idealism + undercapitalization + interpersonal friction = ~3 years.

Connect back to Session 2 on lifecycles. The "3-year cliff" is real and has a shape: the founders' enthusiasm carries the first year, the cracks show in the second, and the third year either institutionalizes or collapses. Every tradition we look at tonight has a version of this curve.

Saint-Simon (1760–1825)

French aristocrat → revolutionary → speculator → prophet.

Argued that society should be reorganized around industry and science, governed by engineers and producers — not landlords or clerics.

"Each according to his capacity, each capacity according to its works." Hierarchy of merit, not birth.

Less of a commune-builder than the others. Matters because:

  • Frames socialism as a question of who runs the economy, not just who owns it
  • Direct ancestor of technocratic & state-socialist traditions
  • Disciples (Enfantin, Bazard) tried colony experiments after his death
Saint-Simon is the odd one out — not really a commune guy, more of a "let's reorganize France" guy. I include him because his line of thought leads to state socialism, technocracy, and eventually to the kibbutz movement's Histadrut/state-builder wing. He's also the source of the meritocratic version of socialism that competes with the egalitarian version.

Étienne Cabet (1788–1856)

French lawyer in exile in London. Wrote Voyage en Icarie (1840) — utopian novel of the country of Icaria.

Best-selling. Cabet's followers ("Icarians") demanded the real thing.

Bought land in Texas, then Nauvoo, Illinois (1849), then Iowa, then California. Communities ran for ~50 years total.

"From each according to his abilities, to each according to his needs." — attributed to Cabet, Louis Blanc, and others before Marx famously used it (1875)
Two things to flag. First, Icaria is the longest-lived of the utopian socialist experiments — half a century. That's worth noticing. Second, the famous "from each, to each" formula didn't originate with Marx. It floats around in 1830s-40s French socialism. Cabet uses it. Louis Blanc uses it. Marx picks it up and canonizes it.

This table is doing a lot of work — it's the empirical record. The pattern is hard to miss: the secular communities lasted years, the religious ones lasted generations. Shakers, Amana, Hutterites — religious commitment provides a filter, a glue, a succession mechanism, and a meaning-frame. Whether that's replicable without religion is one of the central questions of every secular commune since.

Oneida & the Shakers — quick aside

Oneida (1848–1881)

  • Perfectionist Christianity — heaven achievable on earth
  • "Complex marriage" — every adult member married to every other
  • "Mutual criticism" — group feedback rituals (look up: pre-figures encounter groups, Mao-era struggle sessions, modern group therapy)
  • Dissolved into a joint-stock company that still exists (Oneida silverware)

Shakers (1747–today)

  • Celibate. Membership by conversion only.
  • Peak: ~6,000 members across 19 villages
  • Famous for: furniture, music, architecture, gender-equal leadership
  • 2 members alive in 2026 at Sabbathday Lake, Maine

Religious communalism solves the succession problem in opposite ways: total reproductive control vs. forced exogamy.

I include these because they're going to come up. Oneida invented "mutual criticism" — a structured group feedback ritual that everyone since who's done group therapy or T-groups owes a debt to. Shakers chose celibacy and recruited from outside, which works for centuries but has a known failure mode: the recruiting pool dries up. Both are religious solutions to the problem of how communes reproduce themselves.

The Marxist takedown

Marx & Engels coined "utopian socialists" as a put-down in the Communist Manifesto (1848) and developed the critique in Engels' Socialism: Utopian and Scientific (1880).

The argument: utopians design the future from their armchairs. They appeal to reason, philanthropy, the goodwill of capitalists. They build islands. They lose.

"To make a science of socialism, it had first to be placed upon a real basis." — Engels, Socialism: Utopian and Scientific, 1880
"They reject all political, and especially all revolutionary action; they wish to attain their ends by peaceful means, and endeavor, by small experiments, necessarily doomed to failure…" — Marx & Engels, Communist Manifesto, 1848
Engels' book is short and worth reading. The critique has teeth: utopian experiments are islands; capitalism is a system; islands get drowned by systems. The Marxist alternative is not "build a better island" but "take state power and remake the system." For our purposes tonight, this is the crossroads — and we're about to go right toward Kropotkin, who answers "no, actually, federated islands." Hold the tension.

What the utopians got right

  • Imagination is a precondition. You cannot build what no one has described.
  • Concrete experiments produce real data — failure modes that pure theory can't predict.
  • The structural ideas traveled — labor rotation, common storehouse, the Minimum, mutual feedback rituals.
  • They named the problem — alienation, isolation, atomization — decades before sociology did.
Resist the Marxist dismissal. Some of these experiments were genuinely productive — Oneida ran for 30 years and produced silverware your grandmother ate from. The structural innovations are everywhere. And the act of writing down a detailed alternative is not nothing — it's a precondition.

What the utopians got wrong

  • Open membership without shared values → fast collapse (Owen, Brook Farm)
  • Charismatic founder dependency → succession crisis on death (Cabet, Noyes)
  • Undercapitalization → 3-year cliff
  • Treating the colony as the unit of change while ignoring the surrounding political economy
  • Not having a religion, when religion was doing the work

The next tradition we'll meet — the anarchists — accepts most of these critiques and tries to answer them differently than Marx did.

The cleanest summary of utopian failure modes. Each of these gets answered differently by what's next. Anarchists answer "the political-economy problem" with federation instead of state-seizure. Religious communalists answered the values + succession problems with theology. The kibbutz answered the undercapitalization problem with a state that backed them.

Block 2

The Anarchist Tradition

Federated communes, mutual aid, no state — and the revolutions where they tried it.

The anarchist tradition is the one that takes communalism most seriously as a total social form — neither retreat (utopians) nor state-capture (Marxists) but federation from below. It's also the tradition that produced the largest-scale real-world experiments — the Spanish revolution, the Makhnovshchina, the Paris Commune. We're going to spend ~50 minutes here.

What "anarchism" means here

  • Not chaos. Not "no rules." Not bomb-throwing.
  • An-archē — without a ruler. Order without domination.
  • Three commitments most strands share:
    • Voluntary association as the unit of social life
    • Federation, not centralization, when scaling up
    • The means must prefigure the ends
"Anarchy is order without power." — Proudhon
Reset the term. Most people walked in tonight thinking anarchism = chaos. The tradition is in fact obsessed with order — just order produced by association from below rather than imposed from above. The "prefigurative" commitment is worth lingering on: the means must look like the ends. You don't build libertarian socialism with a Bolshevik party. This will matter when we hit Bakunin's break with Marx.

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865)

French printer. First person to call himself an anarchist (1840).

What is Property? (1840) — the answer is on the next slide.

Develops mutualism: workers organized into federations of producer cooperatives, exchanging at cost via labor-time, financed by a "People's Bank."

Distinguishes property (rent-extracting) from possession (use-rights).

"Property is theft."— Proudhon, 1840
"To be governed is to be watched, inspected, spied upon, directed, law-driven, numbered, regulated, enrolled, indoctrinated…"— Proudhon, General Idea of the Revolution, 1851
Proudhon is the spine of European anarchism. The property/possession distinction is essential and is exactly what land trusts and most modern cooperatives operationalize: you can use it, you can't extract rent from it. Mutualism is the moderate wing — it's pro-market, pro-cooperative, anti-rent. Bakunin and Kropotkin will push past this toward common ownership.

Mikhail Bakunin (1814–1876)

Russian aristocrat turned revolutionary. Imprisoned, escaped Siberia eastward through Japan and the US.

Pushes Proudhon's mutualism toward collectivism: means of production held in common, but distribution still by labor.

Famously broke with Marx in the First International (1872) — argued any "dictatorship of the proletariat" would become the dictatorship of the proletariat by a new ruling class.

"Liberty without socialism is privilege and injustice; socialism without liberty is slavery and brutality."— Bakunin, Federalism, Socialism, Anti-Theologism, 1867
Bakunin's prediction of how Marxism would degenerate into a new ruling class was published in 1873 and looks prophetic in retrospect. The split with Marx is the original schism in modern socialism — state vs. federation, vanguard vs. direct action. Almost every later left-wing argument re-runs this fight.

Peter Kropotkin (1842–1921)

Russian prince. Geographer. Naturalist. Revolutionary.

Rejected his title at 12. Did fieldwork in Siberia. Imprisoned in France and Russia. Returned to Russia in 1917 — disgusted by Bolshevism, died in 1921.

Two foundational books for us:

  • The Conquest of Bread (1892) — the picture of a free society
  • Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution (1902) — the scientific case

Kropotkin is the central figure of tonight's anarchist block. He gives the tradition:

  • A biological/anthropological grounding
  • A positive program (federated communes)
  • An economic blueprint (anarcho-communism)
  • And the moral psychology behind all of it
Spend a beat on Kropotkin's biography. The fact that he was a Russian prince who renounced his title and became the leading scientific anarchist is itself important — class betrayal is a structural ingredient in most of these movements. He's also the rare 19th-century radical who comes off as kind in his writing. People who met him used the word "saint." That tonal quality matters and survives in his books.

Mutual Aid — the argument

Kropotkin's response to Social Darwinism (Huxley, Spencer):

  1. Darwin's "struggle for existence" was misread as between members of a species. It's mostly against the environment.
  2. Within species, cooperation is the dominant survival strategy.
  3. He documents this across animals (insects, birds, mammals), then "savages," then medieval cities, then modern guilds and unions.
  4. Therefore: communism is not against human nature — it is human nature, suppressed by state and market.

This is the first systematic biological argument for cooperation. Modern evolutionary biology vindicates large parts of it (kin selection, reciprocal altruism, eusociality).

Mutual Aid is the most under-read foundational text in social thought. The whole 20th century would have been less stupid if more people had read it. The argument matters because it inverts the framing: defenders of capitalism always say "we'd love to share but human nature." Kropotkin says: actually, sharing is the human nature, and capitalism is the deviation. Modern evolutionary bio supports a chunk of this — not all of it, but enough.

Kropotkin in his own words

"Don't compete! — competition is always injurious to the species, and you have plenty of resources to avoid it!" That is the tendency of nature, not always realized in full, but always present. That is the watchword which comes to us from the bush, the forest, the river, the ocean. Therefore combine — practice mutual aid! — Kropotkin, Mutual Aid, 1902, Chapter II (paraphrasing the lesson he draws from animal behavior)
"The mutual-aid tendency in man has so remote an origin, and is so deeply interwoven with all the past evolution of the human race, that it has been maintained by mankind up to the present time, notwithstanding all vicissitudes of history." — Kropotkin, Mutual Aid, Chapter VIII
Read the first quote out loud. It lands differently spoken. The second is the thesis of the book in one sentence. If anyone in the room wants to argue with Kropotkin, this is where to do it — these are testable claims and the modern literature on cooperation is mixed-but-largely-supportive.

Anarcho-communism — the positive program

  • Property: abolished. Means of production and consumption goods held in common.
  • Distribution: "from each according to ability, to each according to need" — Kropotkin pushes past Bakunin's labor-token version.
  • Governance: direct democracy in the commune; federation across communes by recallable delegates.
  • Scale: communes the size of a quarter or village, federated regionally and globally.
  • Labor: abolished as wage-labor; rotation between manual and intellectual work; 4–5 hours a day suffices.

In The Conquest of Bread, Kropotkin literally calculates how many hours of work the population of Paris would need to feed and house itself. It's a startlingly concrete book.

The Conquest of Bread is the answer to "but what would your society actually look like?" Kropotkin walks through agriculture, industry, housing, clothing, and time-use. He is the rare anarchist with a spreadsheet. This is the book that influenced the Spanish anarchists when they actually had to run things.

Gustav Landauer (1870–1919)

German-Jewish anarchist. Translator of Whitman and Kropotkin.

Argued that the state isn't a thing to be smashed — it's a relationship between people, and we leave it by relating differently.

Briefly minister of culture in the Bavarian Soviet Republic (1919). Killed by Freikorps within weeks.

Direct influence on Buber, on the kibbutz movement, and on what we'd now call prefigurative politics.

"The state is a condition, a certain relationship between human beings, a mode of behavior… we destroy it by contracting other relationships, by behaving differently toward one another."— Landauer, Weak Statesmen, Weaker People, 1910
Landauer is the bridge between European anarchism and the kibbutz movement. Buber idolized him. His shift — from "smash the state" to "withdraw from the state by living differently" — is the move that makes communalism possible as a strategy distinct from revolution. It's also why he matters to us: we're not planning to seize state power. We're planning to live differently.

Federation as the alternative to the state

The anarchist answer to "but how does society scale?":

  • Local commune: face-to-face, direct democracy
  • Regional federation: recallable delegates with bound mandates
  • Continental + global: nested federations of federations
  • Coordination by voluntary agreement; no enforcement above the local level

The model: Swiss Jura watchmaker federations of Bakunin's era, the medieval communes Kropotkin documents, and (later) the Spanish CNT.

Federation is the critical structural concept. Modern people often miss this because we conflate "no central state" with "no large-scale coordination." Anarchists from Proudhon onward distinguish coordination from command. Recallable delegates with bound mandates ≠ representatives. They cannot vote their conscience; they vote their commune's instructions.

Three real revolutions

Anarchist communalism wasn't only theory. It ran territory.

  • Paris Commune — March–May 1871, 72 days, ~2 million people
  • Makhnovshchina — Ukraine, 1918–1921, ~7 million people at peak
  • Spanish Revolution — Catalonia & Aragón, 1936–1939, ~8 million people

All three crushed by external military force. None failed internally first.

This slide does important work: it puts the anarchist tradition's empirical record on the table. These weren't 50-person colonies — these were millions of people running large industrial and agricultural economies. Each ended in military defeat, not internal collapse. That's a meaningful pattern.

The Paris Commune (1871)

  • March 18: Parisian National Guard refuses to disarm; revolutionary government declared
  • 72 days of self-rule under siege by the French government from Versailles
  • Mostly Proudhonist + Blanquist + early Marxist; relatively few avowed anarchists, but anarchist structure
  • Innovations: recallable delegates capped at workers' wages, separation of church and state, women's clubs, abolition of night work in bakeries
  • "Semaine sanglante" (Bloody Week), May 21–28: ~20,000 Communards killed by the French army

Marx wrote The Civil War in France defending it. Bakunin saw it as vindication. It became the founding myth of nearly every later revolutionary tradition.

72 days is the answer to "how long can a horizontal society last under siege?" The Commune is short on positive achievements and long on innovations under attack. The bloody week is the ancestor of every massacre of revolutionaries that followed. We remember it because both Marx and Bakunin claimed it, and because the structural innovations — wage caps, recallable delegates — shaped everything that came after.

The Makhnovshchina (1918–1921)

  • Nestor Makhno's anarchist army holds southeastern Ukraine for ~3 years during the Russian Civil War
  • ~7 million people at peak under "Free Territory" administration
  • Land socialized, redistributed to peasants on use-rights basis
  • Volunteer army, elected officers, soviets without Bolshevik party control
  • Fought Whites, Germans, and (eventually) Bolsheviks. Crushed by the Red Army in 1921.

Most under-taught major experiment in 20th-century political history. Read: Voline, The Unknown Revolution; Skirda, Nestor Makhno.

The Makhnovshchina is omitted from almost every Western history of the Russian Revolution because it doesn't fit either Cold War narrative — it's neither Bolshevik nor White. It's the largest pure-anarchist administered territory in history and it ran for three years under sustained military assault from multiple directions. Its destruction by the Red Army is the moment Bolshevism reveals its character to the libertarian left.

The Spanish Revolution (1936–1939)

  • July 1936: Franco's coup fails in Catalonia, Aragón, Valencia
  • CNT-FAI anarchists collectivize land and industry over weeks
  • ~7 million people in collectivized regions; 60% of Catalan industry under worker control
  • Aragón: ~450 collectivized villages, money abolished in many of them
  • Crushed by Franco (with German/Italian help) by 1939; also attacked from inside by Stalinist republicans (Barcelona, May 1937)

First-hand sources to give participants:

  • Orwell, Homage to Catalonia
  • Sam Dolgoff, The Anarchist Collectives
  • Gaston Leval, Collectives in the Spanish Revolution
  • Ken Loach, Land and Freedom (film)
Spain is the most studied of the three because anarchists wrote it down and Orwell witnessed it. The Aragón collectives are the closest historical analog to what people in this room are designing — small-scale agrarian collectives federated across a region, with a participating but uncoercive relationship to a wider economy. Read Dolgoff. The economic detail is remarkable.

Anarchist tradition — the score

  • What worked: federation as a real coordination mechanism; recallable delegates; the link between values and structure was tight; under siege, they didn't internally collapse
  • What didn't: military vulnerability; over-reliance on charismatic figures (Makhno); difficulty handling internal heterogeneity (anarchist vs. Stalinist republican in Spain)
  • Theoretical legacy: mutual aid, prefiguration, federation, the means/ends commitment — all alive in 2026

Most contemporary attempts at horizontal organization (Occupy, Standing Rock, contemporary co-ops, Rojava) are downstream of this tradition.

Note Rojava as the live contemporary example. Open Murray Bookchin's libertarian municipalism as a pointer if anyone asks — that's the direct ideological lineage of Rojava and a 20th-century update of Kropotkin that's worth knowing about. We won't cover it in depth tonight.

Block 3

The Kibbutz Tradition

A 20th-century synthesis of labor Zionism and German-Jewish communalism — the most successful instance of agrarian commune-building, and the one with the most complicated arc.

Kibbutz is one tradition among several. I'm presenting it briefly because it gets disproportionate airtime in commune discussions just because it's the most recent large-scale example most people have heard of. We'll be honest about both the achievements and the entanglements.

The intellectual roots — three figures

A.D. Gordon

"Religion of labor." Manual work on the land as spiritual practice — a Jewish answer to Tolstoy.

Ber Borochov

Marxist labor Zionism. Material analysis: diaspora Jews lack a normal class structure; the project is to build one through productive labor.

Martin Buber

Picks up Landauer. Paths in Utopia (1949): the kibbutz as "the experiment that did not fail."

Plus practical influence from Tolstoyan anarchism, Russian narodnichestvo, and Kropotkin himself — read by the founders.

The three roots map cleanly: Gordon = the spiritual ground, Borochov = the political-economic ground, Buber = the philosophical ground. Buber's link to Landauer is the through-line back to European anarchism — kibbutz is not theoretically isolated, it's a downstream of the same ideas we just covered.

Degania, 1910

  • Twelve people on the south shore of the Sea of Galilee
  • Founded as a kvutza ("small group") — deliberately small, intimate, no hierarchy
  • Common purse, common labor, common dining
  • Established the structural template that scales (with modifications) for 80 years
"We were a community of seekers… we wanted to live by what we believed, not write about it."— Joseph Baratz, founding member of Degania
Twelve people. The number matters — Dunbar-style small group, where consensus is achievable face-to-face. The kvutza model is the original kibbutz. The later "kibbutz meuhad" model scales it up and trades intimacy for capacity.

Distinctive structural features

  • Common purse — no member salary; needs met collectively
  • Common dining hall — central social institution; meals together as ritual
  • Children's houses (controversial) — children slept and were raised collectively rather than nuclear-family
  • Labor rotation — direct echo of Fourier; manual and administrative work shared
  • General assembly — weekly all-member decision-making
  • Hired-labor taboo — members work the kibbutz; bringing in wage labor is a values violation

Note how many of these features are direct implementations of ideas from Blocks 1 and 2.

Pause on the children's houses — this is the most controversial feature and it's the one that didn't survive. Most kibbutzim moved children back to family housing in the 70s-80s. Worth asking the room: which of these features feels transferable? Which feels like an artifact of 1920s ideology?

The arc — peak (1910–1985)

  • By 1948: ~125 kibbutzim, ~5% of Jewish population in Palestine, but disproportionate share of military and political leadership of the new state
  • By 1980s: ~270 kibbutzim, ~3% of Israel's population, ~33% of agricultural output, ~9% of industrial output
  • Embedded in the Histadrut (labor federation) and party structures — receiving substantial state subsidies
  • Internal differentiation: Hashomer Hatzair (Marxist), Ihud (Labor Zionist), Kibbutz Dati (religious)

This isn't an island experiment — it's a movement institutionally entangled with a state. That's both why it scaled and why it eventually unraveled.

The big difference from every prior tradition: the kibbutz was state-supported. Cheap loans, protected markets, military integration. That's the answer to "how did this one not collapse in 3 years?" — but it's also the answer to "why did it unravel when the state stopped supporting it?"

The arc — crisis and privatization

  • 1985: Israeli hyperinflation crisis. Kibbutz movement carrying ~$5B in collective debt; bailed out by government with strings attached
  • 1990s–2000s: wave of hafrata (privatization) — differential salaries, individual budgets, members own homes, hired labor accepted
  • By 2010, ~75% of kibbutzim had moved to "renewed" (privatized) model. ~25% remain "shitufi" (collectivist)
  • Underlying drivers: generational exit of children; ideological hollowing of Labor Zionism; integration of Israeli economy into global capitalism

A communal form can be successful for ~75 years and still convert to its opposite under economic and cultural pressure. Worth sitting with.

This slide is where the lesson lives. 75 years is a long time. Long enough to raise three generations. And the form still converted. Why? Because the kibbutz was always entangled with a wider economy that it didn't control, and when that wider economy moved, the kibbutz moved with it. This is the central cautionary tale for anyone designing a commune embedded in capitalism. State support cuts both ways.

Honest reckoning

  • Kibbutzim were core to the Zionist settlement project and to Israel's military-territorial expansion. Many were established on land from which Palestinians were displaced in 1948.
  • The "redemption of the land through labor" ideology had a specific meaning in a settler-colonial context that the European utopian sources didn't share.
  • This is not separable from the structural achievements. A serious teaching of communalism has to hold both at once.

For depth: Gershon Shafir, Land, Labor and the Origins of the Israeli-Palestinian Conflict; Zeev Sternhell, The Founding Myths of Israel.

I keep this slide brief but I do not skip it. The kibbutz is the most successful agrarian commune of the 20th century AND it was an instrument of a settler-colonial project. Both true. A course about designing communes that didn't address this would be teaching a sanitized history. Take questions if they come; don't dwell.

What kibbutz contributes to our analysis

  • Scale at length — the form actually ran an agrarian-industrial economy for decades
  • Labor rotation worked at scales much larger than Fourier imagined
  • The common dining hall as a social institution proved load-bearing
  • State entanglement is a double-edged sword — it enables scale and creates the conditions for unraveling
  • Children's houses mostly didn't take, even within ideologically committed communities — a data point about family
Quick review. These are the transferable findings. The takeaway for participants designing their own commune: rotation works, common eating works, total collectivization of childcare is a hard sell, and your relationship to the surrounding political economy is a structural choice that will shape your fate.

This is the lens that has the cleanest empirical signal across the whole record. Open membership = fast death. Tight membership filter = long life. The interesting question is not whether to filter — it's how to filter without becoming a cult or replicating exclusion you find immoral. Hold this for the workshop.

This is the lens that determines geopolitics. Withdrawal vs. federation vs. vanguard vs. state-partnership are radically different strategies and they produce radically different outcomes. Most failed communes failed because they couldn't decide on this lens, or they chose one strategy verbally and a different one in practice.

Cross-cutting patterns

  1. Religious communities outlast secular ones — by an order of magnitude. Why?
  2. Membership filter strength predicts longevity — across the entire record
  3. Charismatic founders are a liability at succession — the founder's death is a crisis
  4. State entanglement scales but binds — kibbutz is the cleanest case
  5. Common dining works wherever it's tried — small finding, robust signal
  6. Total collectivization of childcare doesn't take — even in committed communities
These are the empirical findings I'd defend. Each of them has counterexamples but the central tendency is strong. When you write your values statement tonight, treat these as the constraints reality has already imposed on what you're free to design.

What travels — what doesn't

Travels well

  • Common dining
  • Labor rotation (with limits)
  • Recallable delegates
  • Mutual criticism / structured feedback
  • Use-rights distinction (Proudhon)
  • The Minimum (Fourier)

Doesn't travel

  • Total childcare collectivization
  • Charismatic founder dependency
  • Religious bonding without religion
  • Open membership
  • Total dependency on a host state
  • Phalanx-scale architecture (1,620!)

This list is your toolbox for the workshop.

Concretely: when you write your values statement, you're licensed to import from the left column and you should be skeptical of the right column — not because they're wrong in principle, but because the historical record says they're hard.

The deeper question

If religious communities outlast secular ones — and you're building a secular community — what plays the role that religion played?

Hold this question for the workshop. It's the single hardest design question you'll face.

I want to leave the room with this question. It's not rhetorical and I don't have a clean answer. Some options people have tried: explicit ideology (kibbutz), shared aesthetic / culture (Burning Man-adjacent communes), shared work (Mondragón — but that's next week), shared practice (zazen-based sanghas). None replace religion fully. All do some of the work religion did. Your values statement is partly an attempt to answer this question.
Workshop

Your Values Statement

Identify the traditions you most align with. Draft the document that will guide every later decision.

We have ~15 minutes. The full draft will be a homework continuation. Tonight we just want to get something on paper. Pen and paper, please. No laptops.

What a values statement does

  • It's the load-bearing document when you have to make a hard call later
  • It tells a prospective member what they're joining (and not joining)
  • It makes the implicit explicit so disagreement is about substance, not vibes
  • It's shorter than you think — one page, ideally

Reference: read Twin Oaks' "Bylaws," the Bruderhof's "First Law," the Camphill "Pledge." All short. All do load-bearing work.

Stress: short. The temptation is to write a manifesto. Don't. The values statement should be readable in 90 seconds and useful in a fight.

Seven prompts

  1. Property: What's held in common? What stays personal?
  2. Decision-making: Consensus, majority, council, sortition? When can someone decide alone?
  3. Membership: How does someone become a full member? How do they stop being one?
  4. Labor: Is work shared, rotated, specialized, paid, unpaid?
  5. Outside world: Are you a demonstration, a withdrawal, a federation node, a state-partner?
  6. Conflict: What's the process when members fundamentally disagree?
  7. Succession: What persists if the founders leave or die?

For each prompt: one or two sentences. Don't aim for completeness; aim for a position.

These are the prompts. Each prompt corresponds to one of the lenses (with conflict and succession added because they're where actual communes break). Tell people: pick the one that scares you most and start there.

Tonight: 12 minutes of writing

  • Choose three of the seven prompts — the ones you have the strongest take on right now
  • Write two sentences for each. Position, not justification.
  • Then circle one tradition from tonight that your answers most resemble
  • Then write one sentence on where you differ from that tradition

12 minutes. Phones off. Pen and paper. Go.

Set a timer. Walk the room while they write. After 12 minutes, ask for two volunteers to read what they wrote. Don't workshop it tonight — the goal is a first articulation, not a polished document. Tell them they'll iterate this every week.
